What to Do When a Debt Is Not Yours

Receiving a collection notice for a debt you do not recognize is unsettling. It might be a billing error, a case of mistaken identity, or even identity theft. Whatever the cause, you have the right to dispute a debt you do not owe and to demand verification before paying anything. This educational guide from Debt Help Form walks through the steps to investigate, dispute, and protect yourself, including what to do if fraud is the underlying problem.

Why wrong debts show up

Debts are routinely bought, sold, and transferred between collectors, and information can become garbled along the way. Common causes of a debt you do not recognize include simple clerical errors, two people with similar names, a debt that was already paid, an account belonging to a relative, or fraud committed in your name. Understanding the range of possibilities helps you approach the situation methodically rather than assuming the worst or, conversely, ignoring it.

Because errors are common, you should never feel pressured to pay simply because a collector says you owe. The Fair Debt Collection Practices Act gives you the right to request validation, and the CFPB at consumerfinance.gov explains how that process works. Do not pay before verifying

It can be tempting to pay a small disputed amount just to make the problem go away, but doing so may have consequences. In some situations, making a payment or even acknowledging a debt can be treated as confirming it is yours, which can complicate a later dispute. Before sending any money, confirm that the debt is genuinely yours and accurately stated. Paying a debt that is not yours rarely solves anything and may invite further confusion.

Slowing down also protects you from scams. Fraudulent callers count on quick, fearful payments. By insisting on written verification first, you create space to evaluate the claim calmly. This general guidance applies whether the debt is an honest error or something more serious. If you are unsure how a payment might affect your rights, an attorney can explain the implications before you act on any demand.

Request debt validation in writing

Your strongest first move is to request validation. Under the FDCPA, if you ask in writing within thirty days of the collector's initial notice, the collector must pause collection until it provides verification of the debt, including the amount and the name of the original creditor. This forces the collector to substantiate the claim rather than simply asserting it. A validation request is not an admission; it is a reasonable demand for proof.

Send your request promptly and keep a dated copy, using a delivery method that confirms receipt. Clearly state that you dispute the debt and are requesting verification. Avoid including unnecessary personal details. If the collector cannot validate the debt, it generally should not continue collecting it. The CFPB at consumerfinance.gov offers sample dispute letters that you can adapt to make sure your request is clear and complete.

  • Dispute in writing within thirty days if possible
  • State clearly that you do not owe the debt
  • Request the original creditor's name and the amount
  • Keep dated copies and proof of delivery

Gather your own evidence

While you wait for validation, collect any records that support your position. These might include bank statements, prior payoff confirmations, correspondence, or proof of identity that distinguishes you from another person with a similar name. The more documentation you assemble, the stronger your dispute will be. Even if you cannot prove a negative outright, organized evidence shows that you have investigated the matter in good faith and can rebut specific claims.

Keep everything together in one place, whether a physical folder or a digital archive. Note dates and sources for each document. If the debt stems from an account you never opened, your evidence will overlap with the identity-theft steps discussed later. Thorough preparation pays off, because it lets you respond precisely if the collector or a credit bureau asks for support during the dispute process.

Check your credit reports

A disputed debt may also appear on your credit reports, so review them carefully. You are entitled to free reports from the nationwide credit bureaus, and consumer.ftc.gov explains how to obtain them. Look for the account in question, note the reported balance and dates, and check for any other unfamiliar accounts that could signal a broader problem. Spotting additional unknown entries is an important clue, especially when fraud may be involved.

If you find an inaccurate account, you can dispute it directly with the credit bureau, which must investigate under the Fair Credit Reporting Act. Keep copies of your dispute and any responses. Correcting credit-report errors is a separate process from disputing the collection itself, but the two often go hand in hand. Addressing both helps ensure that an erroneous debt does not quietly damage your credit while you work to resolve it.

When identity theft is the cause

If the debt resulted from someone using your information, you are dealing with identity theft, which has its own response path. The FTC operates IdentityTheft.gov, accessible through consumer.ftc.gov, where you can report the theft and generate a recovery plan and an identity-theft report. This official report is a powerful document, because it can support requests to block fraudulent accounts and to stop collection on debts you did not incur.

Acting quickly matters. The sooner you document the fraud, the easier it is to limit the damage and demonstrate that the account is not yours. Identity theft can affect multiple accounts at once, so a thorough review is wise. This guidance is educational and not legal advice; if the situation is complex or large in scale, a licensed attorney experienced in identity theft can help you navigate the recovery steps effectively.

Filing reports and notifying authorities

For identity theft, consider creating an FTC identity-theft report at IdentityTheft.gov and, in many cases, filing a report with local law enforcement. Together these can form an identity-theft report that creditors and collectors are required to honor in certain ways. Notify the collector in writing that the debt is the result of identity theft, attach your report, and request that collection stop. Keep copies of everything you send and receive.

You may also place a fraud alert or a security freeze on your credit files to make it harder for the thief to open new accounts. The credit bureaus and consumer.ftc.gov describe how these tools work. Reporting fraud does not instantly erase every consequence, and resolving identity theft can take time, but a documented, official report is the foundation for clearing fraudulent debts from your name.

  • Create a report at IdentityTheft.gov
  • Consider a police report for documentation
  • Notify the collector in writing with your report attached
  • Place a fraud alert or security freeze on your credit

Responding to the collector

When you contact the collector to dispute, be clear and factual. State that the debt is not yours, that you are requesting validation, and, if applicable, that it stems from identity theft. Do not provide payment or admit liability. Keep the tone professional, and put your dispute in writing so there is a record. A collector who receives a proper dispute generally must verify the debt before continuing to pursue it.

If the collector cannot or will not validate the debt yet keeps contacting you, that conduct may raise FDCPA concerns. Document each interaction with dates and names. Persistence in collecting an unverified or fraudulent debt is exactly the kind of issue that regulators and attorneys take seriously. Your written record will be invaluable if you need to escalate the matter through a complaint or a legal claim.

Escalating an unresolved dispute

If a dispute stalls, you have escalation options. Submit a complaint to the CFPB at consumerfinance.gov, which routes it to the company and tracks a response, and report the matter to the FTC at consumer.ftc.gov. Your state attorney general's office may also accept complaints. These channels create an official record and can prompt a company to address an inaccurate or fraudulent debt more seriously than informal calls alone.

Escalation is not a guaranteed fix, and it does not by itself resolve a legitimate balance. But for debts that genuinely are not yours, persistence through official channels is often effective. If the collector files a lawsuit over a debt you dispute, respond promptly and consider consulting a licensed attorney. Ignoring a lawsuit can lead to a default judgment, even when you had a strong defense that you simply never raised.

Protecting your information going forward

Whether the issue was an error or fraud, take steps to safeguard your information afterward. Monitor your credit regularly, be cautious about sharing sensitive details, and consider keeping a security freeze in place if identity theft was involved. Strong, unique passwords and careful handling of mail and documents reduce future risk. These habits are general best practices that help prevent a recurrence of the problem you just worked to resolve.

Stay alert to follow-up scams as well. Sometimes fraudsters target people who have already been victimized, posing as recovery services. Be skeptical of unsolicited offers to fix your credit for a fee, and rely on official resources at consumer.ftc.gov. Protecting your information is an ongoing effort, and the diligence you build during a dispute can serve you well long after the immediate matter is settled.

Watching deadlines and timelines

Disputes work best when you act promptly. The FDCPA gives you a thirty-day window after a collector's initial notice to request validation and have collection paused, so missing that window can make your position harder, though it does not eliminate your ability to dispute later. Credit-bureau disputes and identity-theft reports also move faster and more smoothly when filed soon after you discover the problem rather than weeks or months down the line.

Mark important dates as soon as a notice arrives, and note when you send each letter so you can track responses. If a collector ever files a lawsuit over a debt you dispute, the summons will carry its own strict deadline that you must not miss. Staying on top of timelines keeps your options open and signals that you are engaged. When deadlines feel tight or confusing, a licensed attorney can help you prioritize the right next step.

Avoiding common mistakes

A frequent mistake is ignoring the notice entirely, hoping it will go away. Silence can allow an erroneous debt to escalate, harm your credit, or lead to a lawsuit. Another mistake is making a partial payment to appease the collector, which may inadvertently acknowledge the debt. A third is disputing only by phone without any written record. Each of these can weaken your position when you actually have a valid defense.

Instead, respond promptly, dispute in writing, request validation, and keep meticulous records. If identity theft is involved, document it through official channels. These measured steps protect your rights far better than either panic or avoidance. Remember that this is general educational guidance, not legal advice, and that a licensed attorney can help you avoid missteps in a complicated or high-stakes dispute over a debt you do not owe.

When to seek professional help

Some situations call for professional assistance. If you are sued, if the identity theft is extensive, or if a collector continues pursuing a debt you have clearly disputed, a licensed consumer-protection attorney can evaluate your options and represent your interests. Many offer free initial consultations, and some handle these cases on contingency. Professional help is especially valuable when the dollar amounts are large or the facts are tangled.

Moving forward with confidence

A debt that is not yours can be resolved, but it usually takes prompt, organized action rather than a single phone call. Dispute in writing, demand validation, gather evidence, check your credit, and, if fraud is involved, file an official identity-theft report. Each step builds a record that supports your position. While no outcome can be guaranteed, exercising your rights methodically gives you the best footing to clear a debt you genuinely do not owe.
