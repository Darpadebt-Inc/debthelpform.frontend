How to Stabilize Your Credit Score While Paying Down Debt

Paying down debt is good for your long-term credit health, but the path there isn't always smooth—balances shift, old accounts close, and a single missed payment can undo months of progress. The good news is that credit scoring models reward a small set of behaviors consistently. Here's how to keep your score steady, or even improve it, while you work through a payoff plan.

Know what's actually moving your score

The two largest factors in most credit scoring models are payment history and credit utilization—how much of your available revolving credit you're using at any given time. Together they typically make up the majority of your score. Length of credit history, the mix of account types, and recent credit applications play smaller roles. When you're paying down debt, the behaviors that matter most are: paying on time every cycle, and lowering the percentage of your credit limits that you're using.

Manage utilization deliberately as balances drop

A common guideline is to keep total revolving utilization under roughly 30%, with lower generally being better. As you pay down credit cards, your utilization naturally improves—but two things can undercut that progress: closing a paid-off card (which reduces your total available credit and can push utilization on your remaining cards higher) and letting balances creep back up on cards you're not actively targeting. If you're not sure whether to close a paid-off account, consider that keeping it open with a small or zero balance often supports a lower utilization ratio than closing it.

Protect your payment history above everything else

A single payment reported 30 or more days late can have an outsized effect on your score and can stay on your credit report for up to seven years. If you're funneling extra money toward one high-priority debt, make sure every other account still gets at least its minimum payment on time. Automatic payments for the minimum due, with manual extra payments toward your target debt, is a common way to avoid accidental late payments while still making progress.

Check your credit reports and dispute errors

You're entitled to a free copy of your credit report from each of the three nationwide bureaus—Equifax, Experian, and TransUnion—through annualcreditreport.com. Review each report for accounts that aren't yours, balances that don't match your records, or payments incorrectly marked late. If you find an error, you can file a dispute directly with the credit bureau, which is generally required to investigate within 30 days. Correcting a reporting error can matter as much to your score as any single payment.

Build budgeting guardrails so progress sticks

Stabilizing your score while paying down debt is easier when your budget has a buffer. A small emergency fund—even a few hundred dollars—can prevent a surprise expense from turning into a new credit card charge or a missed payment. Tracking your due dates in one place (a calendar, a spreadsheet, or your bank's bill-pay tools) reduces the chance that a payment slips through the cracks simply because it was forgotten rather than unaffordable.
