How to Stabilize Your Credit Score While Paying Down Debt

Your credit score doesn't move in a straight line while you pay down debt—it responds to specific events along the way. Rather than treating your score as a mystery, it helps to know what typically happens at each stage of a payoff journey, and which of your own choices push it up or down. Here's a stage-by-stage look at keeping your score steady from the moment you start paying down debt to the moment you finish.

Early on: a temporary dip is common, and usually short-lived

If you're restructuring debt—applying for a lower-rate card, a loan, or working with a credit counselor—you may see a small, temporary drop in your score from a hard inquiry or a new account lowering your average account age. This is normal and typically fades within a few months as long as you keep making payments on time. The goal in this phase isn't to avoid every dip; it's to avoid compounding it with a missed payment or maxed-out card at the same time.

Mid-journey: utilization becomes the lever you control most

As balances start coming down, your credit utilization ratio—the percentage of your available revolving credit you're using—typically improves along with it, which tends to help your score. You can accelerate this by making more than one payment per month on cards you're actively using, since utilization is generally calculated from your statement balance at a point in time, not your balance after you pay it off at the end of the month. Paying a card down mid-cycle, before the statement closes, can lower what gets reported even if you eventually pay the same total amount.

Watch your credit mix and inquiries during this stretch

Scoring models also give some weight to the mix of account types you carry (revolving credit like cards versus installment loans like auto or personal loans) and to how many new credit applications you've made recently. This doesn't mean you should open new accounts purely to diversify your mix—that usually isn't worth the hard inquiry and the reduction in average account age. It does mean you should be selective about applying for new credit while you're mid-payoff, since each hard inquiry can cause a small, short-term dip.

If you have help: authorized user status and co-signed accounts

Some people paying down debt are also managing shared accounts, such as being an authorized user on a family member's card or a co-signer on a loan. These accounts report to your credit file just like your own, for better or worse—an authorized user can benefit from a primary cardholder's positive payment history, but a co-signed account with a missed payment can hurt both parties' scores. If you're in either arrangement, it's worth periodically checking that the account is being reported and managed the way you expect.

Near the end: closing accounts and finishing strong

Once a debt is paid off, think before closing the account. Closing a paid-off credit card removes that available credit limit from your utilization calculation, which can raise your utilization ratio on your remaining cards even though your total debt hasn't changed. If the account has no annual fee, there's often little downside to keeping it open and unused. In the final stretch of a payoff plan, continue monitoring your credit reports at annualcreditreport.com to confirm that paid-off accounts are being reported accurately as closed or paid in full, not left showing an old balance.
